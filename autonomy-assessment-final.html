Autonomous IT Maturity Model

Who decides and who acts. Not how many scripts you have or how mature your monitoring is.

Level 0 — Chatbot
The system answers questions. Humans make every decision and take every action.
Level 1 — Assistant
The system recommends. Humans approve and execute. Decisions follow predefined logic with no exceptions.
Level 2 — Agents
The system acts—but only with human sign-off. Conditional execution with approval gates at every step.
Level 3 — Advanced Agents
The system executes bounded actions automatically. Humans define the guardrails; the system operates inside them.
Level 4 — Expert Agents
The system handles specialized, multi-step workflows end-to-end. Human oversight shifts from execution to policy and exception review.
Level 5 — Agent Ecosystems
Agents coordinate with other agents. The system orchestrates across domains with minimal human intervention.
Question 1 of 5

Signal Quality

Can you suppress, deduplicate, enrich, and correlate alerts reliably?

The foundation of autonomous IT is a signal worth acting on. If your system is still generating noise that requires a human to sort through before anything meaningful happens, autonomy hasn't started yet.

0–1
High alert volume, manual triage dominant, no consistent suppression or correlation
2–3
Consistent correlation in place, meaningful incidents being created, deduplicated to reduce noise
4–5
Correlation actively drives automated decisions—signals trigger action, not just awareness

Question 2 of 5

Context & Investigation

Can the system explain what's happening and what to do next without a human assembling the picture?

This is where most organizations stall. Data exists, but it lives in disconnected tools. A mature context layer links symptoms to services, surfaces likely causes, shows blast radius, and surfaces similar past incidents—so the system arrives at an investigation conclusion, not just a dashboard.

0–1
Investigation is fully manual, engineers correlate across tools by hand
2–3
Some summarization or categorization exists, but prioritization and RCA still require human judgment
4–5
The system produces summaries, RCA, impact mapping, similar incidents, and recommended next actions automatically

Question 3 of 5

Execution Capability

Can the system take action across tools and verify the outcome?

Recommendations without execution aren't full autonomy. This dimension measures whether your system can move from insight to action by triggering runbooks, updating tickets, making changes, and confirming the result.

0–1
No execution capability, or ad hoc scripts with no governance
2
Executes with human approval at each step
3
Bounded auto-execution for defined, low-risk action classes
4–5
Playbook discovery, generation, and execution at scale across ITSM and automation tooling

Question 4 of 5

Governance & Safety

Do you have the controls in place to let the system act and to know what it did?

Governance isn't what slows autonomy down; it's what makes autonomy possible. Without consistent policy controls, audit trails, and approval workflows, your team won't trust the system enough to let it act—and they shouldn't. Execution previews and confidence scoring accelerate adoption by making the system's reasoning visible before it acts.

0–1
No consistent controls—actions are ad hoc, ungoverned, or undocumented
2
Approvals exist but aren't standardized across workflows or teams
3
Policy-based automation in place for defined safe-to-run action classes
4–5
Mature controls—RBAC, audit logs, confidence scoring, execution previews—support broad autonomy

Question 5 of 5

Operationalization

Is agentic work embedded in how your team actually operates or is it still a proof of concept?

Agents running in a sandbox don't reduce your MTTR. This dimension measures whether agentic work is actually part of your live incident workflow and whether your team can prove it with numbers.

0–1
Agentic work exists in pilots or demos, not in production incident workflows
2–3

4–5
Agents are embedded across the full incident lifecycle; noise reduction, MTTR, and manual effort are tracked regularly
